Product orgs,built like products.

Jose Platero builds product organizations the way product people build products: as systems with operating models, feedback loops, and version numbers. The newest version thinks with AI. This is the story, told straight, no fluff.

Jose Platero · Director, Product, Design & MarTech · Air Canada | Aeroplan · Toronto
Chapter one

I loathe travel. So I vowed to fix it.

The stake was personal before it was professional. Fifteen years across TELUS, Shaw Media, agencies, and Aimia's innovation lab, where the job was running 100+ lean experiments and the education was watching most of them fail fast enough to matter. Then Air Canada, 2019, and the chance to work on the thing I'd been complaining about my whole traveling life.

Here's the failure part, because every true story has one. When we rethought aircanada.com's home experience, I forgot to think like a product person. We treated it like a redesign. It wasn't. Product lines had outgrown the page, and what it needed was a rethink of what the page was for. We drew that line late. The work became a Webby honoree, and the lesson became a rule I now say out loud in every kickoff: draw the line before you ship, not after.

The Aeroplan relaunch was the other proof point. Member experience across web and app, partnerships with Uber, Starbucks, and Marriott. Active membership doubled. Multiple Freddie Awards. The real win was quieter: the operating habits the team kept after the launch pressure ended.

Chapter two

The pivot: my product is the system now.

Somewhere between scaling the org past forty-five people and teaching AI certifications at BrainStation, the job changed shape. The products still ship. But the thing I actually build, the thing I'd show you first, is the operating model: agents that draft the PRDs and user stories, automate the executive reporting, audit initiatives, and plan capacity. Governed, repeatable, used across the team. The manual prep work is what got replaced, not the judgment.

The results are the argument. Getting a new team to its operating baseline used to take a quarter; it now takes days. Campaign turnaround went from months to minutes when marketing got a self-serve platform instead of an engineering queue. And Compass, the design system I founded, is becoming an AI-powered product-building platform: components and tokens AI tools can read and build with, so a prototype comes out on-brand the first time.

One rule holds it together: nothing rolls out to the team that I haven't run myself first. My own work runs on the same machinery, research agents that brief me every morning and a knowledge system an AI can actually operate. When one of my own pipelines failed silently, the lesson became policy: every system ships with a watchdog, because a system reporting success is grading its own homework.

Chapter three

The launch I'm proudest of didn't need me.

There's an inflection point in every product career: the day the job stops being the products and starts being the people who ship them. Past forty-five people across four practices, I stopped hiring hands and started hiring and developing a manager layer, because an org that routes every call through one person isn't an org, it's a bottleneck with meetings.

The proof is the status program relaunch. The biggest change to the program since the Aeroplan relaunch itself, 9M+ members moving to spend-based earning, and it was run day-to-day by one of my senior managers. My job was the operating system it ran on and the air cover it needed. The launch landing without me in every room is the point: that's what the manager layer is for.

The same thinking shows up in how teams start. Getting a new team to its operating baseline used to take a quarter of tribal knowledge and hallway archaeology. Now it takes days, because onboarding is a system, not a rite of passage. I give ownership early and real, and I expect artifacts over updates. People grow faster when the work is actually theirs.

Chapter five

While I sleep, it works.

Everything this page claims about operating models has a live counterpart. Every morning at 7:05, before I am awake, launchd fires a headless pipeline: four research agents read the world through four lenses, write their briefs into the archive, and a script compiles the best of it into one email. By the time I am on my bike, the day's intelligence is a podcast in my ears.

And because a system reporting success is grading its own homework, an independent watchdog on separate infrastructure checks the outcome, not the log: did the files land, did the email actually arrive in the inbox. That rule has a scar behind it. The original scheduler once skipped three days while reporting green.
